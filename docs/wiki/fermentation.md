# Fermentation

## Summary

Fermentation is a metabolic process in which microorganisms — primarily yeasts and bacteria — convert sugars into other compounds such as alcohol, lactic acid, or carbon dioxide. It is one of the oldest human technologies, used for food preservation, flavor development, and more recently for industrial production of chemicals and pharmaceuticals. The process was given a scientific explanation by Louis Pasteur in 1857.

## Key Concepts

- **Definition**: anaerobic (no oxygen required) conversion of sugars by microorganisms
- **Three main types**: alcoholic fermentation, lactic acid fermentation, acetic acid fermentation
- **Key organisms**: *Saccharomyces cerevisiae* (yeast), *Lactobacillus*, *Acetobacter*
- **Oldest evidence**: ~7000 BCE, Jiahu, China
- **Scientific basis established**: Louis Pasteur, 1857
- **Applications**: food/drink, preservation, pharmaceuticals, biofuels

## Details

### Types of Fermentation

| Type | Organisms | Products | Examples |
|------|-----------|---------|---------|
| Alcoholic | Yeasts | Ethanol + CO₂ | Beer, wine, bread |
| Lactic acid | Lactobacillus | Lactic acid | Yogurt, kimchi, sauerkraut |
| Acetic acid | Acetobacter | Acetic acid | Vinegar |

### Scientific History

Before the 19th century, fermentation was understood only empirically. Justus von Liebig argued it was a purely chemical, non-biological process. Louis Pasteur's 1857 experiments established the microbial basis, showing that removing microorganisms halted fermentation entirely. This was foundational to germ theory.

### Industrial Scale

Modern industrial fermentation uses bioreactors to control temperature, pH, oxygen, and nutrient levels for maximum yield. Applications include:

- Antibiotics (penicillin was the first industrially fermented pharmaceutical)
- Amino acids for animal feed
- Citric acid (used widely in food and cleaning products)
- Bioethanol as a fuel source
